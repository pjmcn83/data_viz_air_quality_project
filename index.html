Introduction

The global state of air quality monitoring

Air pollution is the greatest environmental threat to human health, causing an estimated seven million premature deaths worldwide each year. The most dangerous of these pollutants is PM2.5 (particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers), which leads to and exacerbates numerous health conditions and impairs cognitive development in children.

In 2023, only 9% of globally reporting cities and 7% of countries achieved the WHO annual PM2.5 guideline value of 5 µg/m³, highlighting the need for action across the globe to reduce air pollution. Long-term monitoring of air pollutants is a fundamental step in addressing air quality issues. It allows for more informed health interventions, the provision of economic incentives and the implementation of policies.

However, inequalities emerge in the way global air quality is measured. This site explores the spatial distribution of air quality sensors worldwide, and investigates the potential mechanisms driving gaps.

Why the gaps?

Exploring the factors contributing to monitoring disparities

The disparities in air quality monitoring are driven by a combination of factors, many of which are embedded in historical inequalities. The following three factors are the main contributors to the divides we see.

Political

South Korea has the best sensor coverage in the world, with 88% of the population living within 5 km of an air quality sensor. Just next door, North Korea has none. The border between these two countries represents one of the starkest data divides on Earth — driven not by geography, but by governance...

Policy-driven monitoring in Chicago

Chicago benefits from a dense, well-funded sensor network embedded across its neighbourhoods. Cairo, one of the world's most air-polluted cities, has comparatively sparse monitoring infrastructure. What we can't see, we can't act on.

Limited capacity in Africa

Regions in Africa are among the most populated in the world, but little air measurement data is available. This is largely due to economic and infrastructure constraints — sensors are expensive to procure, maintain and calibrate.
