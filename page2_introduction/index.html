Introduction

The global state of air quality monitoring

Air pollution is the greatest environmental threat to human health, causing an estimated seven million premature deaths worldwide each year. The most dangerous of these pollutants is PM2.5 (particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers), which leads to and exacerbates numerous health conditions and impairs cognitive development in children.

In 2023, only 9% of globally reporting cities and 7% of countries achieved the WHO annual PM2.5 guideline value of 5 µg/m³, highlighting the need for action across the globe to reduce air pollution. Long-term monitoring of air pollutants is a fundamental step in addressing air quality issues. It allows for more informed health interventions, the provision of economic incentives and the implementation of policies.

However, inequalities emerge in the way global air quality is measured. This site explores the spatial distribution of air quality sensors worldwide, and investigates the potential mechanisms driving gaps.
